Experiences why I am Obsessed with a Personalized Space

Most people avoid it, but there is a space I can never prevent. Since I was a kid, I have emphasized finding a “space that fits me.” The stability and comfort of the space that fits my height have always been unique. For example, the seat above the bus wheel was a place anyone tried to avoid, but I felt most comfortable there. Thanks to my petite height, the space fits my body perfectly, and if I could lean on the window, it could not be more perfect. The best time to ride the bus is in the morning when I go to school. The morning sun gently came through the window, and in winter, a subtle breeze entered when I opened the window slightly, wearing a cozy puffer. This space gave me more comfort and safety than any other space. Moreover, when the bus reached Namsan Mountain, covered with snow, I went past my stop just to enjoy the scenery from my favorite seat. It might be an unfavorable spot to others, but it was a psychological shelter beyond physical comfort for me.

I tried to figure out why I liked this space and recalled some memories. As a small child, I would lie under my bed or desk, read a book, or play an architectural game called Minecraft. Then, I found comfort in small spaces, like when I put my feet up to the legs of the furniture. Since then, I have realized how important a space that fits to reach my foot is. Even when brushing my teeth, I felt stable only when I put my foot on the toilet and squatted one leg. This experience shifted my perspective on space and eventually led to the “Ordinary Village” project. This project explored a new approach to space by bringing a puppy into my home. My family always kept the door open because of the puppy, and that is when I thought about what the door could mean: a big moving wall for the puppy. It occurred to me that furniture legs for people could be a pillar for a puppy, which is how the “Puppy Plan” idea was born. This concept aimed to distinguish between human and animal space and to explore how space could meet the needs of different scales. Just as the “space that my foot can reach” I experienced as a child was my safe and comfortable space, I realized that even my dog was exploring her favorite space.

Moreover, it made me think about how to reflect the psychology and preferences of humans and animals through architecture, which has become an essential element of spatial design. Different people, even the same person, may have other preferred spaces depending on the situation and their experience. Like the stability of the space I felt in my childhood and on the seat above the wheel of the bus, I realized that designing the right space for each individual is an important task that provides sensory stability beyond just physical design.
